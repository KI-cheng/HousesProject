MLP Structure Diagram

Design by mAtrIx

This figure shows the structure of our prediction methods.

1. Input layer: receives input of 8 features.

2. Hidden layer: 128->64->32->1

3. Output layer: Finally output a value. The forward propagation of the network starts from the input layer, goes through the hidden layer processing, and finally reaches the output layer. Back propagation is used to adjust the weight of the network during training. The legend illustrates the network components represented by different colors and shapes: white rectangles represent fully connected layers, light blue represents batch normalization layers, and red represents ReLU activation functions.

Scatter Plot of Predicted Values vs Actual Values

Design by mAtrIx

Most of the blue dots are close to the red dotted line, especially in the 20,000 to 60,000 price range, indicating that the model is more accurate in this range. However, in the higher price range (over 60,000), some blue dots can be seen to deviate from the red dotted line, indicating that the model has a certain error in the prediction of high-priced properties, perhaps due to the lack of high-priced housing data, or the model's insufficient generalization ability on high-priced housing data. Overall, the points in the graph show a positive linear trend, that is, as the actual house price increases, the predicted house price of the model also increases, which means that the model is basically able to capture the overall change trend of house price.

Histogram of Prediction Errors

Design by mAtrIx

Concentrated distribution of errors: As can be seen from the figure, most of the prediction errors are concentrated around 0, especially in the range from -2000 to 2000, indicating that the prediction errors of the model are mostly small, and the model is more accurate in predicting the price of most real estate. The shape of the error distribution: The errors appear to be close to a normal distribution (bell curve), most of the prediction errors are concentrated in the center, indicating that the model's predictions are basically accurate, and only a few prediction errors are large. Left-right deviation: The negative error (left) means that the model underestimates real house prices, and the positive error (right) means that the model overestimates house prices. As can be seen from the graph, the distribution of errors is slightly more in the negative (underestimation) region than in the positive (overestimation) region, which may mean that the model is more inclined to underestimate house prices. Special Error: There are fewer data points with large errors in the -8000 to -4000 and 2000 to 4000 ranges, indicating that the model may occasionally have large forecast errors in extreme cases (such as high - or low-priced properties), but these cases are less common.

Heatmap of Error Distribution

Design by mAtrIx

This graph is a heat map of error distribution, showing the relationship between prediction error and actual price. The horizontal axis represents actual prices, and the vertical axis represents prediction errors. The depth of color indicates the frequency, and the color bar displays the range of frequencies, from light yellow (low frequency) to deep red (high frequency).

From the graph, it can be seen that most of the prediction errors are concentrated in the lower range of actual prices, and the error values are relatively small. As the actual price increases, the distribution of prediction errors becomes more dispersed, but the frequency decreases. This indicates that the model's predictions are more accurate in the lower price range, while the prediction errors are larger in the higher price range, and the accuracy of the predictions has decreased. Heat maps help us intuitively understand the predictive performance of models in different price ranges.

Cumulative Error Distribution

Design by mAtrIx

This graph is a cumulative error distribution chart, showing the cumulative proportion of absolute relative error. The horizontal axis represents the percentage of absolute relative error, and the vertical axis represents the cumulative proportion.

From the graph, it can be seen that as the absolute relative error increases, the cumulative ratio rapidly rises and approaches 1 at lower error values, indicating that most predictions have smaller errors. This may mean that the predictive model has high accuracy in most cases, but there may also be some significant errors. The shape of the chart is similar to an S-shaped curve, which is a typical characteristic of the cumulative distribution function (CDF).

Prediction Accuracy Distribution

Design by mAtrIx

This chart is a bar chart showing the distribution of prediction accuracy. The horizontal axis represents the relative error range, and the vertical axis represents the sample size. The figure shows that the relative error of most predictions is between 0-5%, and the sample size is close to 2000, indicating that the model predicts very accurately in most cases. As the relative error range increases, the sample size significantly decreases. There are approximately 600 samples within the 5-10% error range, while the sample size further decreases within the 10-15% error range. The sample size for error ranges of 15-20% and above 20% is very small and can be almost negligible. This indicates that the model has high prediction accuracy in most cases, but may have significant errors in some cases.
